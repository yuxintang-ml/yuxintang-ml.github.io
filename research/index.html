Selected Research

Assessing surface drainage conditions

Â 

Surface drainage at the local scale is often ignored due to the lack of up-to-date fine-scale topographical information. This paper addresses this issue by providing a novel method for evaluating surface drainage at the neighborhood and street scales based on mobile lidar (light detection and ranging) measurements. The developed method derives topographical properties and runoff accumulation by applying a semantic segmentation model (a computer vision technique) and a flow direction model (a hydrology technique) to lidar data. The results suggest that the proposed method can effectively evaluate surface drainage conditions at both the neighborhood and street scales and identify problematic low points that could be susceptible to water ponding.

For more detail, please visit here for the paper: Assessing surface drainage conditions at the street and neighborhood scale: A computer vision and flow direction method applied to lidar data.

Inequalities and disparate impacts during Winter Storm Uri

During Winter Storm Uri in February 2021, which brought historically low temperatures, snow, and ice to the entire state of Texas, the power-generating plant operators resorted to rolling blackouts to prevent wider collapse of the whole power grid when power demand overwhelmed supply. However, little evidence exists regarding the extent to which these managed outages are implemented in an equitable manner. By analyzing community-scale big data (e.g., digital trace and crowdsourced data), we can infer the extent of disparities in storm-related impacts on vulnerable populations. In addition to assessing disparate impacts of the managed power outage, this study also examined the extent of burst pipes and disrupted access to food.

For more detail, please visit here for the paper: Community-scale Big Data Reveals Disparate Impacts of the Texas Winter Storm of 2021 and its Managed Power Outage.

Assessing drainage conditions of roadside channels

The study used mobile LiDAR to capture and extract six critical drainage channel features, which are:

  • Channel depth
  • Channel bottom width
  • Channel side slope
  • Channel longitudinal slope
  • Length of subsurface pipes and culverts
  • Density of subsurface pipes and culverts

The developed automated process can effectively and efficiently evaluate roadside channels, providing an alternative to conventional manual inspection methods.

For more detail, please visit here for the paper: Automating the evaluation of urban roadside drainage systems using mobile lidar data.
